The Parent Move
One honest sentence
You don't need a plan to start. You just need the willingness to leave the door open.

Talking to your teen about paying for college is one of the hardest parts of the entire college process, and one of the most important ones you'll have together. In fact, SoFi research found that 68% of families say discussing student debt is more uncomfortable than talking about sex. If you've been putting off this conversation, you're not alone.

To understand why, and what actually helps, we sat down with Dr. Dave Anderson, a clinical psychologist and Vice President at the Child Mind Institute.

"The sex conversation is difficult," he told us. "But it never feels like you're a failure for your kid. The college money conversation is different."

If you've been dreading the college cost conversation, that's not avoidance. It's love. You've supported your child their entire life. Telling them that the school they're dreaming about may not be financially realistic doesn't feel like a budgeting exercise. It can feel like a broken promise.

But, it isn't a broken promise.
And, it is a budget conversation.
That's part of why we made this series – and why we're starting here.

There's a reason this feels awkward

There's another layer to this that most families don't talk about – often because they're living inside it. Chances are, you've kept the details of your finances private from your kids. Not out of secrecy, but because it's your job to handle the money. Their job is to focus on school and being a kid.

Now college is approaching, and suddenly you need to have a frank financial conversation with a teen who may not be expecting it. That's a difficult transition. But waiting for the conversation to feel easier may just prolong the tension.

"

Delaying rarely helps with the emotional weight of a conversation. It usually just extends it.

How to have a productive college conversation

When these conversations go well, they can really connect you with your teen. Before you sit down, keep three things in mind:

1
Have a plan.

Have one or two points you want to cover, that's it. You're not addressing every part of the college process in this conversation. You're just starting one.

2
Leave the last conversation out of this one.

It's tempting to open with context, "I know this hasn't been easy to talk about..." But doing that can pull you both back into the last disagreement before anything new has been said. Start fresh every time.

3
Say something. Don't ask something.

Questions like "How are you feeling about all of this?" can put your teen on the spot. A simple statement like "I'm here whenever you want to talk about this" keeps the door open without pushing them through it.

If your teen says something that throws you off, try not to take the bait. Teens who feel cornered or uncertain may say something provocative to derail the conversation. That isn't always defiance – it's often discomfort.

Dr. Anderson suggests acknowledging what they said, then calmly, like a good press secretary would, bring the conversation back to what you want to discuss. "I hear you on that, and I do want to come back to the topics I actually came here to talk about." You don't have to follow the conversation down every path.

If your teen shuts down

Don't force the conversation. Consider putting it in writing instead. The format doesn't matter – text message, email, share doc – as long as the information gets exchanged.

One more thing: if you open the door to a college conversation and your teen walks away, that's okay. Some teens won't engage in the moment you bring it up. But they may circle back later – maybe in the car a few days later with a quiet, "fine, I guess we can talk about it." That's not a setback. That's often how these conversations work.

You don't have to be perfect

At some point in this process, your teen may feel disappointed – and they may look to you to make it better. The instinct is to resolve that uncertainty. To reassure them everything will work out. Before doing that, try asking one question first:

Ask this before you say anything else
"Do you want me to listen right now, or help problem-solve?"

It sounds simple, but many parents skip this step and accidentally shut down a teen who just wanted to feel heard.

It can also help to acknowledge upfront that you might not say everything perfectly. "I know I might not say this exactly right, but I'm really trying to understand where you're coming from." Naming it at the start of a hard conversation can make your teen more patient if you say the wrong thing.

What actually helps

When your teen is struggling

Validate the feeling. "I know this is hard."

Stay in it. "I'm here to figure this out with you."

Hold the uncertainty. "We'll find a path. I just can't tell you exactly what it looks like yet."

Teens can sense the gap between what you say and what you actually know. Honest uncertainty tends to land better than false reassurance.

"

What teens remember, years on, is the explicit intentions you showed, whether it landed that way or not. You can say the wrong thing, as long as you're still saying: I want to support you, and I want to work through this.

Dr. Dave Anderson  ·  Child Mind Institute
