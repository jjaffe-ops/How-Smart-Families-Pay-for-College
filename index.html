Starting the conversation you've been avoiding

Talking about college money is harder than almost anything else. Here's why — and what actually works when you finally sit down with your teen.

The Reality
68%
of families find student debt harder to talk about than sex
The Expert
Dr. Dave Anderson
Clinical psychologist, Child Mind Institute
Your Move
One sentence
You don't need a plan. You just need to start.

If you've been putting off the college money conversation, you're not alone — and you're not failing. SoFi research found that 68% of families say discussing student debt is more uncomfortable than talking about sex. The reason is simple: it can feel like admitting you've let your child down.

But it isn't a broken promise. And it is a budget conversation. That's why we're starting here.

Why this feels different

You've kept the details of your finances private from your kids — not out of secrecy, but because it's your job to handle the money. Now college is approaching, and suddenly you need a new kind of honesty with a teen who may not be expecting it.

"

Delaying rarely helps with the emotional weight of a conversation. It usually just extends it.

How to actually have it
1
Have a plan — a small one

Pick one or two points you want to cover. That's it. You're not resolving every part of the college process in this conversation. You're just starting one.

2
Leave the last conversation out of this one

It's tempting to open with context: "I know this hasn't been easy to talk about..." But that pulls you both back into the last disagreement before anything new has been said. Start fresh every time.

3
Say something. Don't ask something.

Questions like "How are you feeling about all of this?" can put your teen on the spot. A statement like "I'm here whenever you want to talk" keeps the door open without pushing them through it.

If they shut down

Don't force it. Try putting it in writing instead. Text, email, a shared doc — the format doesn't matter. As long as the information gets exchanged.

You don't have to be perfect

At some point in this process, your teen may feel disappointed — and they may look to you to make it better. Before you rush to reassure, try asking one question first:

Ask this before you say anything else
"Do you want me to listen right now, or help problem-solve?"

It sounds simple. But many parents skip this step and accidentally shut down a teen who just wanted to feel heard. Naming your uncertainty upfront — "I might not say this exactly right, but I'm really trying" — makes your teen more patient when you stumble.

What actually helps

When your teen is struggling

Validate the feeling. "I know this is hard."

Stay in it. "I'm here to figure this out with you."

Hold the uncertainty. "We'll find a path. I just can't tell you exactly what it looks like yet."

"

What teens remember, years on, is the explicit intentions you showed — whether it landed that way or not. You can say the wrong thing, as long as you're still saying: I want to support you.

Dr. Dave Anderson  ·  Child Mind Institute

Teens can sense the gap between what you say and what you actually know. Honest uncertainty tends to land better than false reassurance.
